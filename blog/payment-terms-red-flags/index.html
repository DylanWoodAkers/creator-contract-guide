Payment Terms That Screw Creators (And What to Demand Instead)

You delivered the content. The brand loved it. It's been posted for weeks. And you still haven't been paid.

Sound familiar? Payment issues are the #1 complaint we hear from creators. And most of the time, the problem started in the contract ‚Äî with vague or brand-favoring payment terms.

Here are the payment clause red flags to watch for, and what to demand instead.

Red Flags in Payment Clauses

üö© Red Flag #1: "Upon completion"

Why it's bad: Who decides when it's "complete"? The brand can delay payment indefinitely by requesting more revisions or claiming the work isn't satisfactory.

What to demand: Payment upon delivery (when you submit the content) or upon posting (when it goes live).

üö© Red Flag #2: Net 60+ for small deals

Why it's bad: Waiting 2+ months to get paid for a $1,000 deal is unreasonable. Long payment terms are designed for enterprise B2B transactions, not creator partnerships.

What to demand: Net 15 for deals under $2K, Net 30 max for larger deals.

üö© Red Flag #3: No late fee provision

Why it's bad: Without penalties, brands have no incentive to pay on time. "Net 30" becomes Net 60, 90, or never.

What to demand: Late fee of 1.5% per month (standard) or flat fee per week overdue.

üö© Red Flag #4: "Payment contingent on performance"

Why it's bad: Unless it's explicitly a bonus structure, tying your base pay to views or engagement shifts brand risk to you. You can't control the algorithm.

What to demand: Fixed fee for content creation. Performance bonuses can be additional, not replacement.

üö© Red Flag #5: No kill fee

Why it's bad: If the brand cancels after you've blocked time, turned down other deals, or started work ‚Äî you get nothing.

What to demand: 50% kill fee before production, 100% after delivery.

The Ideal Payment Structure

Why upfront payments matter:

  • Confirms the brand is serious and has budget
  • Reduces your risk if they ghost
  • Standard practice in creative industries
Pro tip: If a brand refuses any upfront payment, that's a red flag. Legitimate brands with real budgets don't have issues paying deposits.

How to Enforce Payment Terms

Step 1: Invoice immediately

The moment you deliver content, send an invoice. Don't wait for them to ask. Include the due date prominently.

Step 2: Follow up systematically

  • Day 7 past due: Friendly reminder email
  • Day 14: Firmer follow-up, reference contract terms
  • Day 21: Final notice with late fee calculation
  • Day 30+: Formal demand letter, mention next steps

Step 3: Know your options

  • Small claims court: For amounts under $5K-$10K (varies by state), you can file yourself without a lawyer
  • Collections agency: They take a percentage but handle enforcement
  • Public pressure: Last resort ‚Äî sharing experiences on creator communities (be factual, not emotional)
